The intercept value of 282.722 represents the estimated average trip duration when all other predictors are zero. The coefficient for precipitation is 18.933. It suggests that, on average, a one-unit increase in precipitation (in inches) is associated with an estimated increase of 5.013 seconds in trip duration. However, the p-value of 0.913 indicates that precipitation is not statistically significant in predicting trip duration The coefficient for maximum temperature is 1.305, which is also not statistically significant, similar to TMAX.The coefficient for snow is -39.897. This implies that a one-unit increase in snowfall (in inches) is associated with an estimated decrease of 39.897 seconds in trip duration. The coefficients for different seasons (Spring, Summer, Winter) indicate the estimated differences in trip duration compared to the reference season (presumably Fall). For example, trips in Spring are estimated to be longer by 56.670 seconds, while trips in Summer are shorter by 294.468 seconds. Winter trips are shorter by 108.313 seconds, all compared to the reference season. The model’s overall fit is still weak, as indicated by the low R-squared values (0.0128 and 0.00575). These values suggest that only a small portion of the variance in trip duration is explained by the predictors included in the model.The RMSE of approximately 3568.61 indicates the average prediction error of the model on the test data. The model’s low R-squared values, combined with many non-significant predictor variables, suggest that the selected predictors may not adequately explain the variation in trip duration. The model’s limited ability to capture the relationship between predictors and trip duration indicates that other unaccounted factors might influence trip durations more significantly.
